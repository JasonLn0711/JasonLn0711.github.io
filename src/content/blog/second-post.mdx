Open-source intelligence work often starts with a deceptively simple request: collect the right material, summarize it, and surface the signals that matter. In practice, each step adds noise if the pipeline is not deliberate.

## Collection is only the first step

Scraping headlines is not the same thing as building a usable security feed. A good system needs:

- deduplication across sources
- source quality checks
- language normalization
- fast enrichment for topics, entities, or threat themes

Without those layers, the result is a stream of text that still requires too much manual effort.

## Design choices that keep the system lean

For MVP pipelines, I like to keep the architecture small:

1. A scheduler that collects pages and stores raw items
2. A normalization step for article text and metadata
3. Embedding or topic extraction for grouping related reports
4. A review layer that shows why an item appears in a cluster

This keeps the system understandable while still creating leverage for the analyst.

## Why clustering helps

Security and fraud reporting often arrive as fragments. One article mentions phishing infrastructure, another mentions arrests, and a third references the malware family by name. Clustering helps connect those fragments into a more coherent signal.

## Practical lesson

One of the most important design choices is to preserve the raw source alongside the processed output. Analysts need a path back to the original article, not just a model-generated interpretation.

## Minimal code, better review

```ts
type OsintRecord = {
  source: string;
  title: string;
  publishedAt: string;
  clusterId?: string;
  url: string;
};
```

Even simple schemas like this improve traceability because they make the pipeline easier to reason about and extend.

## Closing note

The goal of an OSINT pipeline is not to look intelligent. It is to help someone find trustworthy context faster than they could alone.
